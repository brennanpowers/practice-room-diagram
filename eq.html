EQ Tuning Guide

Carving frequency space for a small mixed-source room
← Layout diagram Sources & references

EQ in a small room with multiple sources is mostly about subtractive carving — getting each instrument out of the others' way rather than boosting things louder. Below is a practical starting setup for the bass amp, guitars, and the M200 vocal channels, plus what to listen for during practice and how to adjust.

The single biggest concept

The mud zone is 200–400 Hz. Bass body, guitar low-mids, vocal warmth, and kick boom all want to live there. In a small room with hard walls, that range piles up and everything sounds congested. The single highest-leverage move across all your instruments is cutting a little 200–400 Hz on every source. Don't boost there. Anywhere.

Source 01 · Bass amp (B1)

Bass owns the bottom — don't let it own the low-mids too

KnobStarting positionWhy
Bass Noon — or slight cut if room sounds boomy Small rooms naturally bloom bass via boundary loading. You don't need amp EQ to add what the room already adds.
Mid Noon to +1 o'clock Note definition lives at ~700 Hz – 1.5 kHz. Adds articulation so individual notes are distinguishable instead of one rumbling blur.
Treble Noon Don't kill the string attack — that's how the bass cuts through.
Low cut / "deep" switch (if present) ENGAGE Rolls off below ~50 Hz where the room can't reproduce cleanly anyway. Major room-cleanup win.

Listen for: can you tell which note the bass is playing? If yes, EQ is right. If everything sounds like one rumble, more mid.

Source 02 · Guitar amps (G1, G2)

Two guitars in one room means they're competing for the same midrange

The fix isn't to make both louder — it's to make them occupy different frequency lanes.

KnobG1 / G2 starting positionWhy
Bass 10 o'clock (slight cut) Cut 100–200 Hz on both. Leave that range for bass guitar.
Mid Noon (then differentiate — see below) Where guitars live. Both sit here, but their tonal centers should differ.
Treble Noon More than this gets harsh in a small room.
Presence / Bright switch Off or low "Presence" boosts 3–5 kHz which is also where vocal intelligibility lives. Don't fight the singers.

Differentiation between G1 and G2 — pick a tonal stance for each:

  • One guitar can be mid-scooped (less ~800 Hz – 1.5 kHz, more "metal" sound)
  • The other can be mid-forward (more ~800 Hz – 1.5 kHz, more "classic rock")
  • They'll naturally separate without fighting

Listen for: can you hear each guitar as a distinct part? If they blend into one mass, the EQ is too similar — push them apart.

Source 03 · Vocals (M200 per-channel + master)

Two-band shelf per channel + 5-band graphic on the master

The M200 only gives 2-band shelf per channel (HIGH @ 10 kHz, LOW @ 80 Hz, ±12 dB) plus a 5-band graphic on the master. Use them this way:

Per channel · CH1 & CH2

KnobSettingWhy
LOW (80 Hz shelf) Cut 25–40% (~5–8 dB cut) Removes proximity-effect boom on cardioid mics + reduces bass-amp bleed in the vocal channel. Single most important EQ move on the entire mixer.
HIGH (10 kHz shelf) Noon to slight boost (~+2 dB) Adds vocal "air" and presence. Don't push past +25%; HF feedback is the worst kind.

Master · 5-band Graphic EQ

BandSettingWhy
63 Hz Cut −6 dB or more Vocals don't need it. Kills room boom.
250 Hz Noon (cut −3 dB if mucky) The mud zone. Try noon, cut if vocals sound congested.
1 kHz Noon Vocal fundamental zone. Don't touch unless feedback.
4 kHz Slight boost (+2 to +3 dB) Intelligibility region. Helps vocals cut over guitars. Watch for feedback before pushing more.
10 kHz Noon Sibilance zone. Boost for air, but be careful.
Troubleshooting

What to listen for during practice and how to fix it

SymptomLikely causeFix
Vocals buried, bass and guitars feel huge Mud zone (200–400 Hz) overloaded Cut LOW shelf on vocal channels MORE; cut 250 Hz on master EQ
Vocals sound thin / harsh Too much HIGH shelf or 4 kHz boost Pull both back to noon
Bass sounds boomy / one-note Too much amp bass or no low cut Engage low-cut on bass amp; cut amp BASS knob
Guitars sound like a wall of noise EQ too similar between G1 and G2 Push them apart — one mid-scooped, one mid-forward
Constant low-frequency feedback 63 Hz or 250 Hz building up Cut those bands on the master graphic EQ further
Constant high-frequency feedback (painful kind) 4 kHz or 10 kHz Cut those on master graphic EQ
One specific feedback note that won't go away A specific room mode Cut the closest graphic band by 6 dB
Philosophy

Less is more

Boost no more than 2 bands by no more than 3 dB each. Cut as much as you want.

Cuts make room. Boosts make noise. In a small room with hard walls, cuts are almost always the right answer.

Limits

What you can't fix with EQ

  • Drum kit volume → cymbal mutes, dynamics, or cymbal brushes.
  • Direct mic bleed → that's geometric (the layout we designed); EQ only trims what's coming through.
  • Feedback caused by gain-staging → re-check that mics are pointing the right way (rear-null toward mains) before EQ-ing the symptom.
